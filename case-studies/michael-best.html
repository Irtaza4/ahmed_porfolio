01 — The Challenge

Rankings existed — but traffic didn't follow

michaelbest.com wasn't starting from zero — it had some existing presence and a real audience. But there was a disconnect: the site was ranking for a growing list of keywords yet converting that visibility into meaningful, sustained organic traffic at nowhere near its potential. The content existed. The links were beginning to accumulate. But the site was leaking value at every level — technically, structurally, and strategically. The challenge wasn't simply to rank — it was to make every existing asset work harder, and then scale from there.

01
Traffic-to-Keyword Mismatch
13,800 keywords ranking but only 21,700 monthly sessions — meaning the site was indexing for a large volume of long-tail, low-traffic terms while missing the mid-volume keywords that actually drive compounding sessions.
02
Backlink Concentration Risk
63,300 backlinks from just 2,800 domains — an average of 22+ links per domain. While the volume was impressive, the link profile lacked diversity and was over-reliant on a small cluster of high-frequency sources.
03
Content Without Conversion Architecture
Pages existed but weren't structured around searcher intent journeys. Content was answering questions without guiding users toward any logical next step — rankings arrived but didn't compound into meaningful business outcomes.
04
Invisible to AI Search
Despite a growing backlink profile and decent keyword coverage, the site had zero presence in AI-generated answers. The content wasn't structured in a way that LLMs could reliably cite — a growing source of discovery traffic being left entirely on the table.
02 — How It Was Done

5 phases. 5 disciplines.
10 months of compounding results.

The diagnosis shaped the strategy. This wasn't about rebuilding from scratch — it was about identifying exactly where value was leaking, fixing those specific failure points, then scaling aggressively on the back of a site that finally worked as it should. Each phase unlocked the next.

PHASE ONE Technical SEO

Stop the Bleed — Diagnosing Why Rankings Weren't Becoming Traffic

The first month was spent entirely in diagnosis mode. The site had keywords ranking but traffic wasn't following — which pointed to something more specific than a lack of content or links. A full crawl analysis revealed the root issue: inconsistent canonical signals were causing Google to index the wrong versions of multiple pages, splitting ranking signals and suppressing click-worthy URLs from appearing in SERPs. Duplicate meta structures and thin page variants were compounding the problem.

Simultaneously, a Core Web Vitals audit flagged significant LCP failures on the highest-traffic page templates — slow server response times and unoptimised above-the-fold assets were directly affecting how Google evaluated the user experience. Every technical fix was prioritised by traffic impact, not just severity score. By month two, canonical architecture was clean, page speed was green, and the site was finally sending coherent signals to Google about what it actually wanted to rank.

Canonical Architecture Fix Duplicate Content Resolution Core Web Vitals — LCP Priority Server Response Optimisation Crawl Signal Consolidation Index Bloat Cleanup Thin Page Merging / Removal Structured Data Validation
PHASE TWO Keyword Strategy & On-Page Rebuild

Shifting the Keyword Mix — From Long-Tail Depth to Mid-Volume Momentum

The existing keyword profile told a clear story: the site was ranking for thousands of highly specific, low-volume terms but almost nothing in the 500–5,000 monthly search volume bracket where the real traffic density lives. The strategy here wasn't to abandon long-tail coverage — it was to identify the mid-volume head terms and subtopic clusters that were just out of reach, and engineer a path to rank for them.

Every existing page was audited against its target keyword cluster. Pages optimised for one intent were often inadvertently ranking for another — creating mismatched signals that confused both users and Google. Title tags, H1 structures, and meta descriptions were rewritten with a strict intent-first framework. High-potential pages that were sitting at positions 8–15 received focused on-page upgrades: richer content depth, stronger internal links from authoritative pages, and structured answer formatting to compete directly for snippets. Within three months, a meaningful cohort of these near-miss rankings had moved to page one.

Mid-Volume Keyword Targeting Intent Reclassification Position 8–15 Uplift Campaign Title & Meta Rewrite Internal Link Equity Routing Featured Snippet Structuring Content Depth Expansion SERP Feature Targeting
PHASE THREE Content Architecture & Topical Authority

Building the Content Ecosystem That Makes Google Keep Coming Back

With technical issues resolved and the on-page foundation strengthened, the focus moved to content strategy — specifically, the kind of content architecture that signals topical authority rather than just isolated relevance. The site had content but it existed in silos: individual pages answering individual questions, with no overarching semantic structure connecting them into a coherent knowledge system that Google's crawlers could map and trust.

A topic cluster model was architected across the domain's core subject areas. Each cluster was built with a definitive pillar page at the centre — comprehensive, authoritative, and targeting a high-competition head term — surrounded by tightly interlinked supporting content covering subtopics, adjacent questions, and use-case variants. This architecture did two things simultaneously: it channelled internal link equity toward priority pages and it signalled to Google that this domain understood its subject matter at depth, not just at the surface. By month six, Google Search Console was showing consistent crawl frequency increases — the domain was being revisited more often, and new content was indexing within hours of publication.

Topic Cluster Architecture Pillar Page Development Supporting Content Production Internal Linking System Topical Entity Mapping Crawl Frequency Optimisation Content Gap Closure FAQ & Structured Q&A Layers
PHASE FOUR Off-Page SEO & Link Profile Diversification

Broadening the Link Profile — Depth Was There, Diversity Wasn't

The existing backlink profile had a specific structural problem: 63,300 total backlinks from only 2,800 referring domains — an average of over 22 links per domain. This meant the site's authority was heavily dependent on a concentrated cluster of high-frequency linking sources. While not harmful, it created a fragility risk and was leaving significant link diversity signals untapped. The off-page strategy in this engagement was less about volume and more about breadth.

The priority was acquiring editorially earned links from domains that had never linked to the site before — expanding the referring domain base rather than deepening existing relationships. Digital PR campaigns were built around genuinely citable content assets: original data, expert commentary, and resource pages that gave other sites a reason to link once and naturally keep referencing. Guest content was placed on authoritative domains in adjacent niches to introduce the brand to new link neighbourhoods. By month nine, referring domain diversity had improved markedly, the Authority Score had reached 38, and the link profile was far more resilient to any single-source dependency.

Referring Domain Diversification Digital PR — Original Data Assets New Domain Acquisition Focus Adjacent Niche Guest Posting Expert Commentary Outreach Resource Page Link Building Link Neighbourhood Expansion Profile Fragility Reduction
PHASE FIVE AI Search Visibility (GEO / AEO)

Engineering Citability — How a Site Becomes an AI's Source of Truth

The final phase focused on a channel that most SEO campaigns completely ignore: AI-generated answers. The challenge here was structural. LLMs don't cite pages because they rank well — they cite pages because the content is formatted in a way that makes attribution natural and reliable. The site's existing content, despite being well-written, wasn't structured with AI citability in mind: answers were buried inside long paragraphs, entity relationships weren't made explicit, and there were no clear fact-anchors that AI systems could extract and attribute with confidence.

Content was systematically restructured across priority pages. Answer blocks were brought to the surface, entities were named and defined explicitly, and citation-signal architecture — including authoritative source references and clear factual statement formatting — was embedded throughout. The platform-by-platform results reflect the strategy's nuance: Google AI Mode generated the most cited pages (220) because of Google's deeper integration with the site's existing indexed content, while Gemini produced the highest raw mentions (82) — indicating strong entity recognition. Total outcome: AI Visibility Score of 23 from zero, with 426 pages now cited across all four major AI search platforms.
